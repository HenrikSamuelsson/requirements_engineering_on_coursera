# Requirements Specifications: Goals and Conflict Analysis

## Module 1 - Goal Creation

### Introduction to Goal Development and Language Conflict Analysis

This course focuses on the **analysis phase** of requirements engineering, following elicitation. The goal is to transform raw stakeholder input into structured, evaluated, and negotiable requirements that can be used to build a realistic and robust system.

The course emphasizes that requirements engineering is **iterative** and involves continuous refinement through elicitation, analysis, documentation, management, and consolidation.

#### Purpose of the Course

After requirements have been elicited, they must be:

* Organized into meaningful structures (e.g., goals, use cases)
* Evaluated in terms of risks and alternatives
* Analyzed for conflicts and inconsistencies
* Refined into a form suitable for implementation

The course introduces techniques to ensure that requirements:

* Reflect real stakeholder needs
* Cover functional, nonfunctional, and **pervasive requirements** (e.g., security)
* Support system design and development

#### Key Activities in This Course

The course centers around four main analysis activities:

##### 1. Goal Development

* Requirements are structured as **goals**
* Helps identify:

  * Missing objectives
  * System intent
  * Alternative ways to achieve outcomes

##### 2. Conflict Analysis and Resolution

* Stakeholder needs may conflict
* Trade-offs must be identified and negotiated

Examples of conflicts:

* Performance vs security
* Cost vs reliability

Goal:

* Reach agreement between stakeholders (or acceptable compromise)

##### 3. Risk Identification and Evaluation

* Requirements must be evaluated for risks, including:

  * Safety hazards
  * Security threats
  * Development risks

Many risks originate as **unknown unknowns** and must be discovered during analysis.

##### 4. Evaluation and Negotiation

Core analysis activities include:

* Identification and resolution of inconsistencies
* Risk assessment and mitigation
* Comparison of alternative solutions
* Selection of preferred options
* Requirements prioritization

#### Important Concepts

##### Iterative Process

Requirements work is not linear:

* Elicit → Analyze → Refine → Repeat

Continuous interaction with stakeholders is required to reach a complete and correct specification.

##### Pervasive Requirements

Some requirements (especially **security**) are:

* Not just “nonfunctional”
* Affect the entire system

These must be considered throughout all analysis activities.

##### Alternatives and Trade-offs

Multiple solutions may exist for the same goal:

* Different implementations
* Different responsibility allocations
* Different automation levels

Each alternative must be evaluated based on:

* Risk reduction
* Impact on nonfunctional requirements
* Feasibility

##### Prioritization

Not all requirements can be implemented due to:

* Cost constraints
* Schedule constraints

Therefore:

* Requirements must be prioritized
* Incremental development must be supported

#### Outcome of the Course

After this course, you should be able to:

* Structure requirements using goals
* Identify and resolve conflicts
* Analyze risks and missing requirements
* Evaluate alternative solutions
* Prioritize requirements for implementation

The result is a **more complete, consistent, and realistic requirements specification**.

#### Group Sessions for Requirements Engineering

##### What are Group Sessions

Group sessions are structured workshops where multiple stakeholders
collaborate to:

* Elicit requirements
* Analyze system goals and constraints
* Negotiate conflicts and trade-offs

They leverage **interaction between diverse participants** to generate
insights that would not emerge in individual interviews .

##### Why Use Group Sessions

* Generate **new ideas and requirements** through discussion
* Reveal **conflicts and inconsistencies** between stakeholders
* Enable **shared understanding of the system-to-be**
* Support **analysis and negotiation activities**, such as:

  * Conflict resolution
  * Risk identification and mitigation
  * Evaluation of alternative solutions
  * Requirements prioritization

Group sessions are particularly useful when moving from raw elicitation
toward **agreement and decision-making** .

##### How Group Sessions Work

###### Structure

* Conducted as **a series of workshops** (often multi-day)
* Include **follow-up activities** between sessions
* Combine:

  * Plenary discussions
  * Breakout groups for focused topics

###### Key Dynamics

* Interaction leads to:

  * Better perception of the domain
  * Improved judgement
  * Creative invention of new solutions

* Stakeholders often:

  * Refine ideas between sessions
  * Return with new insights after reflection time

##### Best Practices (DO)

###### Preparation

* Define a **clear agenda**
* Share agenda with participants in advance
* Define goals (elicitation vs analysis vs negotiation)

###### Facilitation

* Use **breakout sessions** for complex or conflicting topics
* Ensure **structured discussion (moderation required)**
* Allow **thinking time** (not just continuous talking)
* Encourage participation from all stakeholders

###### Execution Strategy

* Run sessions over **multiple days if possible**
* Allow time between sessions for reflection
* Capture:

  * Decisions
  * Open issues
  * Risks
  * Alternatives

###### Follow-Up

* Provide:
  * Organized documentation (single source of truth)
  * Clear action items
* Avoid information overload (e.g., too many emails)
* Use structured storage (folders by topic/date)

##### Anti-Patterns (DO NOT)

* No agenda or unclear purpose
* Uncontrolled discussion (people talking over each other)
* No breaks → reduced effectiveness
* Vague or missing follow-up
* Relying on stakeholders to “email later” (they won’t)

##### Role in Requirements Lifecycle

Group sessions are especially important for:

###### 1. Elicitation

* Discover new requirements through interaction

###### 2. Analysis

* Identify inconsistencies and risks
* Explore system constraints

###### 3. Negotiation

* Resolve stakeholder conflicts
* Evaluate alternatives
* Agree on priorities

##### Key Insight

Group sessions are not just meetings —
they are **decision-making engines** where:

* Ideas emerge
* Conflicts surface
* Trade-offs are negotiated
* Agreements are formed

##### 🪞 Reflection

Compared to interviews, group sessions:

* Increase **creativity and discovery**
* But require **strong facilitation and structure**
* Otherwise they degrade into **inefficient meetings**
